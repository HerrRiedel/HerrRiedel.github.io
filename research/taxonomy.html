A.Consumption The consumption channel: regulation differentially raises firms' marginal costs and therefore relative prices, and consumers reallocate spending across firms — firms with smaller cost burdens gain market share, firms with larger burdens lose it. Requires differentiated goods (so consumers can substitute among firms) but does NOT require exit, strategic interaction, or entry barriers — distinguishing it from the supply-side channels in C.
A.1Demand reallocation from changes in relative prices Rents flow via market-share reallocation on the demand side: regulation shifts firms' relative output prices; consumers substitute toward the firms whose cost burden is smallest relative to the aggregate cost increase; those firms gain market share and capture the rent. Demand-side counterpart to the supply-contraction channels in C.1 and C.2 — same regulation-redistributes-within-industry-rents result, but reached through consumer substitution rather than through rivals being forced out or contracting output.
This paper MWMC Monopolistic competition with differentiated goods, heterogeneous-cost firms, and homothetic demand. Firm profit depends on relative marginal cost (own cost divided by the industry price aggregator), so a cost-raising regulation can benefit a firm whose burden is small relative to the aggregate cost increase — the demand-side counterpart to supply-contraction channels. Cost-raising regulations are classified by how they deform the cost distribution — scale (proportional), location (additive), or shape (vanishing burden on the most productive); shape regulations redistribute market share toward the top of the productivity distribution without requiring exit, strategic interaction, or entry barriers.
— No prior formal model occupies this row.
B.Trade The trade channel: trade policy (tariffs, quotas, liberalization) redistributes rents within an industry. Two sub-channels distinguished by where the within-industry heterogeneity sits — B.1 has uniform within-sector tariff incidence, with within-industry heterogeneity on the contribution/organizational side (who pays for lobbying and in what mode); B.2 has within-sector differential tariff incidence driven by firm productivity heterogeneity, with productive exporters and unproductive import-competing firms holding opposite policy preferences.
B.1Endogenous protection — domestic industry as homogeneous bloc Trade policy under uniform within-sector tariff incidence: all firms in a sector benefit or lose together from the resulting tariff (the "industry-as-bloc" property on the benefit side), and rents flow domestic-vs-foreign at the sector level. Within-industry heterogeneity, when present, operates on the contribution/organizational side rather than the incidence side — WHO lobbies (Mitra 1999: binary organization decision), HOW MUCH each firm contributes (Bombardini 2008: firm-size dispersion → endogenous participation share), and IN WHAT MODE (Bombardini-Trebbi 2012: joint trade-association vs individual).
Political-support / common-agency model of protection Common-agency menu auction over sector-level tariffs (Grossman & Helpman 1994, building on Bernheim-Whinston 1986). Each organized sector lobby submits a contribution schedule mapping the entire tariff vector to a contribution; the government chooses tariffs to maximize a weighted sum of contributions and aggregate welfare. The truthful-Nash equilibrium delivers a closed-form rule t_i/(1+t_i) ∝ z_i/e_i — organized sectors get tariffs scaled by output-over-imports divided by import-demand elasticity; unorganized sectors get import subsidies. Earlier political-support-function variants (Hillman 1982, Findlay-Wellisz 1982) and endogenous-lobby-formation extensions (Mitra 1999, Bombardini 2008) build around the same industry-as-bloc framework.
Canonical
Hillman (1982)HOM Refines Stigler-Peltzman by letting the government's political-support function depend separately on the tariff and the world price rather than on their sum. Tariffs respond asymmetrically to world-price shocks: declines in the world price pass partially through to domestic prices — the tariff rises but never fully offsets. Early formalization of the industry-as-bloc tariff-determination problem.
Findlay & Wellisz (1982)HOM First formal GE model of endogenous tariffs via lobbying. Ricardo-Viner specific-factors economy with two sector-specific factor owners (importable- and exportable-sector lobbies) choosing labor allocated to lobbying in a Cournot-Nash game; the tariff is determined by a reduced-form "tariff formation function" t(L_T, L_K). Industry is a bloc on the benefit side, and because lobbying labor is unproductive, the welfare cost of an endogenous tariff exceeds that of the same exogenous tariff.
Grossman & Helpman (1994)HOM Canonical "Protection for Sale" model. Sector-level lobbies submit contribution schedules to the government in a common-agency menu auction (Bernheim-Whinston 1986); the government chooses the tariff vector to maximize a weighted sum of contributions and aggregate welfare. The truthful-Nash equilibrium delivers the closed-form GH rule t_i/(1+t_i) ∝ z_i/e_i (the ratio of sector output to imports, z_i, divided by import-demand elasticity e_i) for organized sectors, with import subsidies for unorganized sectors — a political-economy analogue of the Ramsey rule for optimal commodity taxation. Workhorse industry-as-bloc model of the literature.
Other
Findlay & Wellisz (1983)HOM Companion to F&W 1982. Section I extends the 1982 specific-factors lobbying model to allow factor mobility across sectors. Section II is a new "bureaucratic-authoritarian" model where the government acts as a faction maximizing public-good output funded by tariff revenue; the optimal tariff exceeds the standard maximum-revenue tariff because the tariff lowers the wage via Stolper-Samuelson (output relative prices determine factor relative prices in general equilibrium) and so stretches the public-good budget further.
Goldberg & Maggi (1999)EMPHOM Canonical empirical test of Grossman-Helpman "Protection for Sale." Estimates the GH structural specification directly (rather than reduced-form) on US 1983 NTB coverage ratios across HS sectors. Recovers a strong welfare weight relative to contributions weight (β ≈ 0.98, α_L ≈ 0.88) and confirms the GH-predicted negative protection-vs-import-penetration relationship within organized sectors that prior reduced-form work had missed.
Mitra (1999)HOM Endogenizes lobby FORMATION in Grossman-Helpman by adding a stage-1 binary organization decision (each sector pays a fixed cost F_i to become a lobby) prior to the stage-2 GH menu auction. Three counterintuitive comparative statics: (1) more contribution-friendly governments produce MORE lobbies but LESS protection per lobby (lobbies cancel each other out); (2) free trade is an equilibrium at BOTH extremes of the government's welfare weight α — at high α (lobbies unprofitable, no sector organizes) and at low α (every sector organizes and their tariff demands cancel out) — rationalizing the US paradox of a contribution-responsive government coexisting with near-free-trade outcomes; (3) greater asset inequality produces more lobbies AND more protection per lobby. Founding paper of endogenous lobby formation, and the direct theoretical antecedent to Bombardini (2008).
Baldwin & Robert-Nicoud (2007) Embeds GH lobbying in a Dixit-Stiglitz monopolistic-competition model with sunk entry costs. In expanding sectors free entry erodes any policy-created rent (so lobbying doesn't pay); in declining sectors sunk costs preclude entry, making quasi-rents appropriable and worth lobbying for. Resolves the empirical "losers' paradox" — declining sectors receive the most protection — without invoking ad-hoc government preferences.
Bombardini (2008)HOM Extends Grossman-Helpman to firm-level lobbying with a fixed contribution cost F: only firms above a size threshold lobby, so GH's binary "organized?" dummy is replaced by a continuous participation share θ_i pinned down endogenously by within-sector firm-size dispersion. Higher dispersion → more participation → higher protection. In head-to-head statistical comparison on US 1986-88 FEC-COMPUSTAT data (non-nested J-tests), GH is rejected against Bombardini's specification while Bombardini's is not rejected against GH. Bridges B.1 to B.2 — within-industry heterogeneity enters on the contribution/participation side, while incidence remains industry-wide.
Bombardini & Trebbi (2012)EMP Asks how firms ORGANIZE lobbying — individually or jointly via a trade association — and tests the answer empirically. Theory: Bertrand duopoly with Singh-Vives substitutability η; firms lobby individually when η is low (differentiated → individual tariffs capture rent privately) and jointly when η is high (substitutable → only sector-wide protection prevents consumer substitution). The model assumes uniform within-sector tariff incidence (Bertrand limit pricing equalizes prices across firms in the sector), so the within-industry margin is the organizational mode, not policy incidence. Empirical: LDA dataset 1999-2001 (3,448 client × SIC entries); Tobit regression finds that more competitive sectors lobby MORE collectively.
B.2Heterogeneous domestic firms Trade policy under within-sector differential incidence: firm productivity heterogeneity (à la Melitz 2003) creates opposite policy preferences between productive exporters (gain from liberalization via foreign-market access) and unproductive import-competing firms (lose from liberalization due to import competition). Papers in this strand assume reciprocity in trade negotiation — home tariff cuts trigger foreign cuts — which is what makes productive exporters care about home policy rather than being indifferent. Rents flow within the domestic industry, from import-competing firms to productive exporters under liberalization.
Heterogeneous-firm trade model with firm-level differential incidence Heterogeneous-firms trade model (Melitz 2003 or Melitz-Ottaviano 2008) embedded in a Grossman-Helpman common-agency lobbying framework with reciprocal tariff-setting between countries. Firms differ in productivity; self-selection into exporting; lobbying is firm-level rather than sector-level. Productive exporters lobby for liberalization (home tariff cuts trigger foreign cuts via reciprocity → better export-market access); unproductive import-competing firms lobby for protection. When both groups lobby, their schedules can offset (Chang-Willmann 2006) yielding tariffs near socially optimal even with a politically active government.
Canonical
Melitz (2003) Canonical heterogeneous-firms trade model. Continuum of monopolistically competitive firms drawing productivity from a distribution G(φ) and paying a fixed export cost, so only firms above a productivity cutoff export. Trade liberalization (lower variable trade costs) expands sales for the most-productive firms and forces exit of the least-productive, with intermediate firms becoming marginal exporters — within-domestic-industry reallocation toward high-productivity producers is the central object of the model.
Kim (2017)EMP Builds LobbyView — 890k LDA reports 1999-2014 matched to COMPUSTAT/Orbis × HS8 tariffs — now the standard firm-level lobbying dataset for the trade-PolEcon literature. Documents that ~70% of US MFN tariff-rate variance is WITHIN HS-4 industry (e.g., cotton with staple ≥28.575mm: 14% tariff; harsher cotton: duty-free), directly contradicting B.1's industry-as-bloc assumption. Theoretical contribution is an extension of Grossman-Helpman with Melitz-Ottaviano demand and 4 heterogeneous firms (2 domestic + 2 foreign); the optimal tariff falls and can become an import subsidy as product differentiation σ → 0. Empirical tests with the new dataset confirm that productive firms lobby more in differentiated industries and that differentiated products receive lower tariffs.
Other
Gilligan (1997)EMP Pre-Melitz political-science paper on within-industry political economy of trade — departs from the rest of B.2 in that within-industry heterogeneity here is in variety position (Lancaster product-circle) rather than productivity, with the mechanism being monopolistic competition within varieties rather than reciprocal liberalization. The argument: under intra-industry trade each firm is effectively a monopolist over its own variety, so adjustment costs concentrate on individual firms and the benefits of lobbying accrue to a single firm rather than being spread across the industry — eliminating the free-rider problem that usually dampens industry-level lobbying. Empirics: Poisson regression of US ITC complaint counts on the Grubel-Lloyd intra-industry trade index (standard measure of two-way trade in a category) at 4-digit SIC 1988-1992 — the coefficient is positive at 1%, so industries with more intra-industry trade file MORE complaints. Striking direct observation: ITC complainants in the sample are ALWAYS single firms (or their subsidiaries), never industry associations — consistent with the firm-level appropriability story.
Chang & Willmann (2006) First explicit fusion of Grossman-Helpman "Protection for Sale" with Melitz heterogeneous firms, under a multilateral-tariff-setting assumption (home tariff cuts trigger reciprocal foreign cuts). Within-industry cleavage: domestic-only firms want higher home tariffs (protection); productive exporters want lower home tariffs (because reciprocity lowers foreign tariffs and improves their export-market access). When BOTH groups lobby, their contribution schedules offset exactly → socially-optimal tariff (even though the government values contributions). Proposes a resolution to the "high α" puzzle in the empirical GH literature: the high estimated welfare weight in Goldberg-Maggi (1999) may be an upward bias from omitting exporter-side lobbying.
Osgood (2016) Theoretical refinement of the Melitz-style "productive firms support trade, unproductive oppose" narrative using Melitz-Ottaviano (2008). Six propositions: from autarky → trade, firm support for liberalization is monotonically decreasing in marginal cost (standard Melitz); but from partial → freer trade, the relationship can INVERT — the most productive firms may OPPOSE further liberalization because intensified compatriot-firm competition in their export markets outweighs the direct gain from better foreign-market access. The set of supporting firms can be a MIDDLE interval [c_PT, c̄_PT] bounded both above and below, rather than "all firms above a cutoff." Provides a theoretical basis for the "non-tariff barriers (NTBs) as RRC" intuition: large incumbents support NTBs that multiply costs proportionally because low-cost firms are barely affected.
C.Production The production channel: regulation alters within-industry resource allocation. Four sub-channels: C.1 — extensive margin (some firms exit or are deterred from entering); C.2 — intensive margin (rivals contract output without exit); C.3 — misallocation via product-market wedges (productive firms inefficiently small, less-productive firms inefficiently large); C.4 — misallocation via labor-market frictions (workers don't flow to highest-MRPL firms).
C.1Exclusion (extensive margin) Regulation changes who participates in the industry: some firms exit (extensive margin) or are deterred from entering. Three sub-rows by how within-industry rents are produced: C.1.a — capture theory (entry restriction creates uniform rents to incumbents, no within-industry differentiation); C.1.b — RRC with exit (asymmetric regulation engineered by some firms drives others out); C.1.c — selection on fundamentals (uniform regulation has differential incidence from firm-level heterogeneity in productivity, quality, or compliance-cost scale economies).
C.1.a — Cartel via entry restriction (capture theory) Cartel via entry restriction (capture theory). A regulator captured by industry imposes entry restriction; incumbents collectively earn rents — uniform within incumbents, so no within-industry differentiation (the row produces rents but doesn't distribute them within-industry). Verbal-economic informal models (Stigler 1971, Posner 1974, Buchanan-Tullock 1975) plus Peltzman's (1976) political-support formal model and Blanchard-Giavazzi's (2001) GE entry-cost formalization. The political-economy foundation that the rest of C.1 (and B.1) builds on.
Canonical
Stigler (1971)EC Founding capture-theory paper. Argues that regulation is acquired by an industry primarily for its own benefit: the state's monopoly on coercion can be "bought" to confer rents on incumbents, and the most fundamental such rent is the power to restrict entry. The dissipation-through-entry argument is central — without entry control, regulatory rents are competed away by new entrants — so entry restriction is the bedrock benefit incumbents seek. No formal model; historical examples (occupational licensing, trucking, airline regulation).
Posner (1974)EC Synthesis of Stiglerian capture theory with Posner's own earlier "Taxation by Regulation" theory (Posner 1971). The TBR extension: regulators use regulated prices to cross-subsidize — taxing some customer groups to subsidize others (e.g., long-distance phone callers cross-subsidize local service) — so regulation can produce industry-customer coalitions rather than just incumbent-vs-consumer cleavages. Distinguishes three political systems (industry, customer, coalition) by which actors are politically organized. Verbal-economic, no formal model.
Buchanan & Tullock (1975)HOMEC Positive theory of why producers prefer direct quantity regulation (quotas) over Pigouvian taxes. Both can correct an externality, but quotas create rents for incumbents while taxes do not — provided entry is restricted. The famous summary line: "regulation in this sense is the directional equivalent of cartel formation." Foundational framework for the regulation-as-cartel-formation literature (Maloney-McCormick 1982, Yandle's Bootleggers-and-Baptists 1983).
Peltzman (1976)HOMEC First formal model of regulator behavior. Regulator maximizes a political-support function M(p, π) that trades off consumer support (decreasing in price) against producer support (increasing in profit); the FOC delivers an equilibrium regulated price strictly between the perfectly-competitive and pure-monopoly levels. Generalizes Stigler from "industry captures regulator" to a continuous bargaining problem in which the regulator weighs both sides. Direct precursor of Grossman-Helpman (1994) contribution schedules.
Blanchard & Giavazzi (2001)HOMEC First formal GE model of the entry-barrier mechanism. Monopolistic competition with elasticity σ(m) increasing in the number of firms m (so more entry → more competition); Nash bargaining over rents with worker bargaining power β; entry blocked by a shadow cost c. The long-run free-entry condition μ(1−β)/(1+μ) = c pins down the equilibrium markup as a function of the entry cost — lower c → more entry → lower markup → lower unemployment and higher real wage. Provides the formal theoretical anchor that Stigler-Posner-Peltzman articulated informally about entry barriers creating incumbent rents.
Other
Djankov et al. (2002)EMPEC Cross-country dataset on entry regulation: number of procedures, time, and monetary cost required to start a business in 75 countries circa 1999. Heavier entry regulation is associated with more corruption and a larger unofficial economy, but NOT with better quality of goods, lower pollution, or better health outcomes — the public-interest justification for entry regulation does not survive the data. Caveat for capture-theory placement: the patterns better support the "tollbooth" interpretation (bureaucrats extracting bribes) than pure capture by incumbents (the authors find no evidence that heavily-regulated industries are more profitable or less competitive).
C.1.b — Raising rivals' costs with exit Raising rivals' costs with exit. An asymmetrically-positioned firm or coalition engineers regulation to differentially burden rivals — either explicitly asymmetric (statutory exclusion, grandfathering) or uniform-on-its-face with differential incidence by design (e.g., a uniform minimum wage that differentially burdens labor-intensive firms, or a uniform pollution-abatement standard whose per-unit compliance cost falls more heavily on smaller plants). The engineering coalition benefits; less-favored rivals exit or are deterred from entering. Theoretical anchor: Williamson (1968) wage-as-RRC two-scale limit-pricing model and Hviid-Olczak (2016) fixed-cost RRC with one rival excluded under forward induction.
Canonical
Hviid & Olczak (2016)HOMFC First fully-specified oligopoly model of fixed-cost RRC. Cost-raising regulation imposes a fixed-cost increase on all firms in an n-firm Cournot oligopoly; convexity of per-firm profit π(n) in the number of rivals is the sufficient condition for the incumbent coalition to profit, even when its own fixed costs rise by as much as rivals' (the headline novelty vs Mason 2002 variable-cost RRC). Forward induction selects the equilibrium with one rival excluded; the surviving incumbents share the rent.
Other
Körber (1995)HOM Formalizes the Salop-Scheffman / Oster intuition of lobbying-for-environmental-regulation-as-RRC with a binary lobbying game. Two firms: U₁ (clean tech, supports regulation) and U₂ (dirty tech, exits if regulation passes). Three pure-strategy equilibria — both lobby, only U₁ lobbies (U₂ silent because counter-lobbying barely moves the success probability), or no lobbying (unresponsive government) — and a two-period extension generates regime switches driven by regulation irreversibility. Direction-classic (productive predator excludes less-productive rival).
Becker & Henderson (2000)EMPFCEC Large empirical study of within-industry effects of the US Clean Air Act on four polluting industries (industrial organic chemicals, metal containers, plastics, wood furniture) using Census of Manufactures + Longitudinal Research Database plant-level data 1963-1992. Identifies two simultaneous asymmetric-regulation mechanisms operating side-by-side: (1) size thresholds (the class-A-polluter cutoff at 100 tons/year), structurally classic RRC but direction-reversed (small plants benefit, large pay); (2) grandfathering (existing plants face only RACT; new plants face stricter LAER), analog to a statutory entry-vintage asymmetry. Findings: plant births fall 26-45% in nonattainment counties; market structure shifts toward less-regulated single-plant firms; grandfathered preregulation plants persist (95-98% survival in attainment areas); the largest-plant industry shows downsizing to stay below the class-A threshold — an intensive-margin response that also gives the paper a foothold in C.2.
Bertrand & Kramarz (2002)EMPEC Departs from the rest of C.1.b in that the mechanism is entry DETERRENCE rather than forced exit, and the protected party is less-productive small shopkeepers — opposite direction to the Williamson / Hviid-Olczak default where productive firms exclude less-productive rivals. Causal empirical study of the French Loi Royer (1973), a zoning regulation requiring regional board approval for any large retail establishment (>1,500 m²) and ostensibly intended to protect small shopkeepers; only ~40% of applications approved 1974-1998. Departement-level panel + IV using political composition (right-wing parties get disproportionate small-shopkeeper votes): stricter approval rates → lower retail employment, higher retailer concentration, higher food retail prices. The authors also document that new-brand applicants are ~5% less likely to be approved than incumbent-brand applicants in the same city, and interpret this as evidence that the approval boards were used to limit competition among large retail chains — empirically validating the Blanchard-Giavazzi (2001) entry-cost-→-markup channel.
Fishback (2005)EMPFC Empirical economic history of US Progressive-Era workplace-safety reform (1869-1930) — workmen's compensation, factory inspection, mine safety. State-level panel + Weibull hazard models for the timing of state-level adoption. Direction-divergent findings across industries: in MANUFACTURING, states with larger establishments adopted regulation EARLIER (consistent with either reformer-driven public-interest pressure OR large firms engineering RRC); in COAL MINING, states with larger mines passed NARROWER regulations with lower enforcement budgets (defensive subversion by large mines facing safety regulation that would hit them harder). The cross-industry direction divergence is the substantive finding — capture-vs-public-interest is not separable empirically with this design alone.
Bailey & Thomas (2017)HOMEMPFCEC First industry-level empirical test of capture-theory within-industry rent predictions using RegData (Mercatus) — a per-industry index of US federal regulatory intensity that counts binding-constraint terms ("shall", "must", "may not", "prohibited", "required") in the Code of Federal Regulations, weighted by how heavily each regulation references that 4-digit NAICS industry. Fixed-effects panel of 215 industries × 1998-2011 merged with Statistics of US Businesses firm-births/deaths/hires data. Findings: a 10% regulation increase produces -0.47% firm births overall, -0.91% large-firm deaths (large incumbents protected from exit), -0.63% new hires; results driven by non-service sectors. Generalization-test complement to the single-regulation C.1.b case-study tradition (Marvel 1977, Bartel-Thomas 1985/87, Fishback 2005, Hviid-Olczak 2016) — establishes the within-industry differential-incidence mechanism as a generic feature of US federal regulation, not an artifact of specific cases.
C.1.c — Selection on fundamentals Selection on fundamentals. Uniform regulation; differential incidence emerges from firm-level heterogeneity (productivity, quality, compliance-cost scale economies). The productivity threshold for survival rises (Hopenhayn 1992 / Asplund-Nocke 2006 framework), so the least-productive firms exit and the industry productivity distribution truncates from below — productive incumbents benefit; marginal firms are displaced.
Canonical
Williamson (1968)MWHOMMC First selection-on-fundamentals insight, motivated by the Pennington case (1965) where large unionized coal operators were alleged to have conspired with the UMW to impose a uniform industry wage. Two-scale Bain-Sylos limit-pricing model: small firms have a higher labor/capital ratio than large firms (a_1 > a_m), so a uniform wage raises small firms' AC more than large firms' AC; small mines exit and large firms raise the entry-preventing limit price and earn rents. Same within-industry direction as later Hopenhayn-style selection (productive firms gain, marginal firms displaced), but with labor intensity as the relevant fundamental rather than productivity. Salop-Scheffman (1983, 1987) generalize the framework to arbitrary cost-raising regulation; the MW-as-RRC literature (Bhaskar-To 1999) extends it to minimum wages.
Maloney & McCormick (1982)HOMEMPFC Theoretical scaffolding + empirical event studies establishing that environmental regulation creates within-industry rents. Section III's intraindustry-transfers result is the key contribution: with heterogeneous-cost firms, uniform environmental regulation raises industry supply price more than the cost of the most-efficient firms, so the most-efficient firms gain WITHOUT requiring entry restriction — productive firms gain, marginal firms displaced. Empirics: two stock-market event studies (OSHA cotton-dust 1974, EPA PSD ruling 1973) showing abnormal returns of 24% and 31.6% for affected industries; within the cotton-dust portfolio, firm-level abnormal returns increase with cotton intensity — consistent with the intraindustry-transfers prediction.
Pashigian (1984)HOMEMPFC First comprehensive empirical study of within-industry effects of US environmental regulation on plant-size distribution. Compares 20 high-pollution-abatement-cost industries (primary metals, paper, cement, refining) to 20 low-abatement-cost control industries, 1958-72 (pre-reg) vs 1972-77 (regulation). Findings: in high-cost industries the number of plants fell relative to controls, mean plant size rose, small plants' market share fell 6-7 percentage points (21-25% of 1972 share) more than predicted, and capital's share of value added rose. Mechanism: economies of scale in compliance — uniform regulation creates fixed compliance costs that hit small plants harder per unit of output, driving the smallest plants out and shifting industry composition toward larger surviving plants.
Hopenhayn (1992)MWHOMFC Canonical heterogeneous-firms industry-dynamics model. Stationary equilibrium with a continuum of firms drawing productivity from a distribution and facing a sunk entry cost c_e plus a per-period operating fixed cost c_f; firms exit when their productivity falls below a state-dependent threshold x*. Comparative static: raising c_f tightens x* (the productivity threshold for survival), so the least-productive firms exit, the industry productivity distribution truncates from below, and the equilibrium market price rises to clear demand. The workhorse selection-on-fundamentals framework for heterogeneous-firms entry/exit dynamics.
Asplund & Nocke (2006)FC Hopenhayn extended to monopolistic competition with strategic interaction. Continuum of differentiated-product firms with heterogeneous marginal costs, sunk entry cost ε, operating fixed cost ϕ. Two key comparative statics: higher ϕ tightens the exit threshold so the most-efficient firms gain and the least-efficient lose (Prop 5, the selection direction parallel to Hopenhayn 1992); higher ε loosens the exit threshold and protects marginal incumbents from exit (Prop 4, the opposite of the fixed-cost effect).
Other
Leland (1979)HOMFC Heterogeneous-quality producers + asymmetric information (consumers can't observe quality, so all sellers charge the same lemons-market equilibrium price). Minimum-quality regulation drives low-quality producers below the standard out of the market and raises the equilibrium price of the surviving (higher-quality) producers. The selection mechanism is by quality rather than productivity, but the within-industry direction is the same — uniform regulation, differential incidence emerging from firm-level heterogeneity, productive (here: high-quality) producers gain at the expense of marginally-displaced (here: low-quality) producers.
Lo Bello & Pesaresi (2025)MWEMPFCMC Quantitative MW paper combining Engbom-Moser wage posting (frictional labor markets) with Atkeson-Burstein-style oligopoly. Minimum wage raises labor costs heterogeneously; low-productivity firms cannot cover their fixed overhead κ and exit, so the number of firms N falls, the variable elasticity of substitution ε(N) falls, and equilibrium markups rise. Foothold in C.1.c (selection-driven exit raising industry concentration) and C.4 (Burdett-Mortensen wage-posting microfoundation). Headline contribution: structural estimation on Italian administrative employer-employee data + optimal-MW counterfactual.
Macedoni & Weinberger (2026)FCMC Heterogeneous-firms model (Melitz-style productivity distribution under CES demand) studying how fixed-cost regulation redistributes within-industry rents. Fixed-cost regulation lifts the productivity threshold ϕ* for survival, so the smallest firms exit and surviving firms gain via demand reallocation — but only firms strictly above the new threshold (sufficiently more productive than the marginal survivor) gain net of the higher fixed-cost burden. Marginal-cost regulation in their CES setup is equivalent to scale-changing regulation in "Regulatory Capture via Relative Prices" — under constant CES markups, no firm of any size supports a uniform marginal-cost increase. The fixed-cost-vs-marginal-cost contrast is the substantive contribution.
C.2Rivals contract output (intensive margin) Rivals contract output along the intensive margin (without exit). Regulation imposes differential marginal-cost burden on firms in homogeneous-good oligopoly with strategic interaction; less-burdened firms re-optimize output upward along residual demand; more-burdened rivals contract output but stay in the market; the market price rises and the less-burdened firms capture the rent transfer.
Strategic oligopoly with cost asymmetry (RRC) Strategic oligopoly with cost asymmetry (raising rivals' costs). Dominant firm + competitive fringe (Salop-Scheffman 1983/1987) or two-scale Bain-Sylos limit-pricing (Williamson 1968); regulation parameter α raises rivals' marginal cost more than the focal firm's. Sufficient condition for profitability (S-S eq. 3): dp/dα > ΔAC_D — the vertical shift in residual demand must exceed the focal firm's own AC shift. Empirical anchors: Marvel (1977) 1833 British Factory Act, Oster (1982) FTC drug regulation, Bartel-Thomas (1985, 1987) OSHA/EPA.
Canonical
Marvel (1977)HOMEMPMC Founding empirical case study of regulation-as-RRC. Marvel calls Britain's 1833 Factory Act "a landmark event both in British social history and in the development of modern industrial organization" — the combination of social-welfare justification with central-government enforcement (the new Home-Office factory-inspectorate system) marks "the beginning of economic regulation as we know it today." Mechanism documented: the Act's hard weekly cap on children's hours (children were 39-51% of textile labor and complementary to adult labor) could not be extended to cover water-supply downtime, so water-powered mills — which lost ~8% output annually to droughts and floods — were forced to shorten operations while steam mills running steady ~69-hour weeks were essentially unaffected. Coalition documented: Manchester/Lancashire steam-powered manufacturers played a major drafting role through the Royal Commission, while Scottish (water-heavy) industry uniformly opposed — the Bradford Short Time Committee complained "The Central Board had a Committee of large Factory Proprietors at their elbows." Empirical evidence: parish-level regressions on 1834-36 Factory Inspectors' enforcement records show that cases brought, charges per case, and fines all rise with parish water-power share — confirming differential incidence.
Oster (1982)MC First theoretical formal model of within-industry RRC via regulation. Departs from the rest of C.2 in that the model uses differentiated-products Cournot/Stackelberg duopoly (Dixit 1979 setup) rather than homogeneous-good cost-side RRC. Three preconditions for rivalrous regulatory investment: (i) differentiated sub-groups, (ii) interdependence (substitutability V>0), (iii) entry/mobility barriers. Headline cost-side result: ∂π_1/∂c_2 = Q_1·V/(2B_2) > 0 — firm 1's profit rises with rival's marginal cost. Case study: FTC Model Drug Product Selection Rule (1979) where the brand-name PMA lobbied along three RRC lines against generic NAPM firms (explicit-physician-request substitution, in-vivo-comparative ANDA testing requirements, manufacturer identification on dispensed-drug labels) — first detailed contemporaneous documentation of regulatory rivalry between within-industry sub-groups.
Salop & Scheffman (1983)HOMMC Founding RRC paper. Dominant firm + competitive fringe in homogeneous-good oligopoly; regulation parameter α raises the fringe's marginal cost more than the dominant firm's, so the fringe contracts supply along its inelastic supply curve and the dominant firm re-optimizes upward along residual demand. Sufficient condition for profitability (eq. 3): dp/dα > ΔAC_D — the vertical shift in residual demand must exceed the focal firm's own AC shift. Positions RRC as an alternative to predatory pricing for antitrust analysis, with three advantages of RRC over predatory pricing: it is profitable even without rival exit (no need to outlast the victim's losses); it has no deep-pocket requirement (the predator may incur minimal cost — e.g., a mandatory product standard); and it is more credible because cost-increasing strategies can often be made structurally irreversible. Companion to Salop-Scheffman 1987, which provides the full formal treatment.
Salop & Scheffman (1987)HOMMC Full formal treatment of variable-cost RRC, companion to Salop-Scheffman 1983. Nine propositions on the dominant-firm-plus-competitive-fringe setup. Key results: Prop 1 restates the sufficient condition dp/dα > ΔAC_D; Prop 2 (counterintuitive) shows profitable cost-raising can LOWER price when the predator has output-market power and the strategy makes residual demand more elastic; Prop 4 fringe profits can RISE rather than fall (the "collusive" flavor of cost-raising strategies); Prop 5 welfare effects are generally ambiguous, with welfare necessarily falling if the predator does not increase output.
Other
Michaelis (1994)HOMMC Formalizes lobbying-for-cost-raising-environmental-regulation-as-RRC in heterogeneous Cournot-Nash oligopoly with a two-party electoral-competition framework (Young-Magee / Austen-Smith). One efficient firm 1 + n identical less-efficient competitors producing a homogeneous good. Key result (Eq. 3): firm 1 lobbies for stricter regulation iff (∂c_k/∂s)/(∂c_1/∂s) > (n+1)/n — more atomized competitors lower the cost-advantage threshold for profitable lobbying. Political-economy comparative statics: unilateral lobbying produces party convergence ("concordance in the extreme"); two-sided lobbying produces party polarization ("maximum product differentiation" as both parties take extreme positions to extract maximum lobbying revenue). Intensive-margin RRC throughout — competitors contract output but do not exit.
Landes (1980)HOMEMP Departs from the rest of C.2 in that regulation creates differential incidence across worker subgroups within the labor market rather than across firms — structurally analogous to firm-level RRC but operating on workers. Empirical study of US state maximum-hours laws for women in manufacturing 1900-1920. Theory (two-group labor market): women face the maximum-hours constraint, men do not; binding constraint on women reduces total labor supply, raises the wage, and substitutes men into women's hours. Empirical finding: women in laws-states worked ~8 fewer hours/week and women's manufacturing employment share fell — crucially differential by nativity, with foreign-born women's employment falling up to 30% in restrictive states while native women's employment was largely unaffected (because native women already worked below the legal limit, so the law didn't bind for them). Landes argues in Section III that states with more foreign-born women in manufacturing passed laws earlier — a pattern he interprets as consistent with anti-immigrant rather than welfarist motivation.
Bartel & Thomas (1985)HOMEMPMCFC Three-equation simultaneous empirical model of OSHA enforcement (accidents, noncompliance, inspections) estimated by 2SLS on 3-digit SIC manufacturing × 22 federal-enforcement states × 1974-78. Introduces the direct/indirect effects decomposition: direct = own-firm safety gain and compliance cost; indirect = competitive advantage from asymmetric within-industry incidence. Headline finding: regulation asymmetrically favors large firms via two channels — compliance asymmetry (economies of scale in capital-equipment compliance favor large firms; t=-35.80) and enforcement asymmetry (the agency inspects small and non-union firms more heavily because they are less politically organized, per Stigler-Peltzman). Direct safety effect is minimal (full compliance would cut injuries only 9.8%) while the indirect rent transfer to politically organized large firms is large — resolving the political-economy paradox of why a seemingly inefficacious agency retains Congressional support.
Bartel & Thomas (1987)HOMEMPMCFC Empirical companion to B&T 1985. Tests whether within-industry cost asymmetries actually generate predator benefit by regressing industry-level price-cost margins and wages on shares of within-industry composition that the framework predicts capture the rents (109 3-digit SIC manufacturing industries × 1974-78). Three dimensions of asymmetry: firm size (LARGE = share in establishments ≥250 workers — captures economies of scale in compliance); region (FROST = share in Northeast + Midwest — EPA's new-source standards burden new plants more than old, and PSD requirements tighten in cleaner regions, both burdening Sun Belt expansion and favoring Frost Belt's older plants); and trade exposure (IMP — import competition prevents domestic price increases and hence rent capture). Identification is inter-industry — industries with higher LARGE/FROST share have higher PCM and wages — from which the paper infers the intra-industry direction (within an industry, large + Frost Belt firms benefit more than small + Sun Belt firms). Results (Table 4): at maximum FROST share, industry profits rise 9.1% and wages 3.8%; at maximum LARGE share, profits rise 2.9% and wages 3.2%; at maximum import share, profits fall 49%.
C.3Misallocation: product-market wedges Misallocation via product-market wedges. Idiosyncratic firm-specific wedges on output, revenue, or factor prices, correlated with firm productivity (more-productive firms face higher effective distortions, hire fewer inputs, produce less; less-productive firms face lower distortions and absorb the reallocated inputs and output share). Within-industry rents flow from productive to less-productive firms; aggregate TFP falls because productive resources end up in less-productive hands.
Heterogeneous-firms framework with idiosyncratic wedges Heterogeneous-firms framework with idiosyncratic firm-specific wedges. Two canonical variants: (i) Hopenhayn (1992) homogeneous-goods framework with decreasing-returns production f(s,k,n) = s·k^α·n^β, α+β < 1; (ii) CES monopolistic competition with σ > 1 (H-K 2009 setup). The wedge (1−τ_i), typically written as a revenue tax with productivity-elasticity γ, is isomorphic to a productivity-correlated marginal-cost markup w_eff = w·(s_i·z_i)^γ on factor prices.
Canonical
Restuccia & Rogerson (2008)HOMMC Canonical misallocation paper. Hopenhayn-style heterogeneous-establishments framework where each establishment faces its own output tax τ correlated with its productivity s. Calibration: with correlated distortions (low-s firms subsidized, high-s firms taxed, aggregate K held constant), a 40% tax produces a 31% TFP loss — almost 4× the uncorrelated case. The output/revenue wedge is isomorphic to a productivity-correlated MC markup on factor prices: high-productivity firms face higher effective marginal cost, hire too few inputs, and are inefficiently small. Generic-wedge framing — τ is reduced-form and can be interpreted as taxes, subsidies, financial frictions, trade restrictions, or regulation.
Hsieh & Klenow (2009)EMPMC Empirical operationalization of the Restuccia-Rogerson framework on plant-level data from China, India, and the US. Key conceptual insight: in frictionless monopolistic competition, revenue productivity (TFPR) is equalized within industries because high-TFPQ plants charge lower prices, so TFPR converges; TFPR dispersion therefore directly measures the magnitude of distortions. Headline counterfactual: equalizing TFPR dispersion to US levels would raise TFP by 30-50% in China and 40-60% in India. In the data, larger plants within Chinese and Indian industries have higher MPL and MPK than smaller plants — implying the larger plants are inefficiently constrained below their optimal scale (this pattern is much weaker in the US) — consistent with R-R's correlated-distortion case. The revenue wedge is isomorphic to a productivity-correlated marginal-cost markup on factor prices.
Other
Hopenhayn & Rogerson (1993)HOM Extends Hopenhayn (1992) to full general equilibrium — adding a representative household with endogenous labor supply and a wage that clears the aggregate labor market — and calibrates on US LRD firm-level data to evaluate the welfare cost of a per-job firing tax τ. Mechanism: misallocation via firm-level inaction bands [n_l(s), n_u(s)] — firms with positive productivity shocks under-hire (afraid of future firing cost), firms with negative shocks over-employ; MPL deviates from 1/p across firms; aggregate productivity falls. Calibration: at τ = 1 year's wages, employment falls 2.5%, productivity falls 2.1%, consumption-equivalent welfare falls 2.8%; 90% of firms have MPL deviations exceeding 5%. Direct precursor to Restuccia-Rogerson 2008: same Hopenhayn-style framework, same misallocation channel, applied to firing-tax regulation. Pure normative welfare-cost analysis with exogenous policy — the paper does not model who benefits or who lobbies for firing taxes.
Guner, Ventura & Xu (2008)HOMMC Quantitative GE analysis of size-dependent regulations (restrictions on large establishments or subsidies to small ones) in a Lucas (1978) span-of-control framework calibrated to US firm-size data. The wedge is correlated with firm size rather than productivity directly, but the within-industry mechanism is the same as R-R 2008 — productive firms forced below their optimal scale, less-productive firms over-allocated inputs, with the usual TFP loss. Quantifies the welfare cost of common policies that effectively cap or penalize large establishments (e.g., regulatory thresholds that exempt small firms).
Bento & Restuccia (2017)EMPMC Extends the R-R 2008 / H-K 2009 misallocation framework to a CES monopolistic-competition setup (following H-K 2009) by endogenizing entrant productivity investment (s_0) and life-cycle productivity growth (g) — making the productivity distribution itself respond to distortions, not just input allocation across a fixed distribution. Key result: productivity-correlated distortions (low-productivity firms subsidized, high-productivity firms taxed) roughly DOUBLE the static-misallocation impact on aggregate TFP, because they discourage productivity investment by all establishments. The revenue wedge (1−τ_i) = (s_i·z_i)^(−γ) is isomorphic to a productivity-correlated marginal-cost markup w_eff = w·(sz)^γ. Empirical contribution: assembles a new 134-country dataset on manufacturing establishment size, finding that average plant size rises strongly with country-level development (income elasticity 0.29) — reversing prior negative-correlation findings.
C.4Misallocation: labor-market frictions Misallocation via labor-market frictions. Labor markets are imperfectly competitive — firms face upward-sloping firm-level labor supply curves and post wages marked down from MRPL. MW interacts with these monopsony rents to reallocate workers from less- to more-productive firms: low-productivity firms lose margin and exit or shrink; high-productivity firms absorb the freed workers; within-industry rents flow from low- to high-productivity employers.
Imperfectly competitive labor markets with wage markdown Firms post wages marked down from MRPL. Finite labor-supply elasticity from amenity preferences (Robinson, Card et al.), wage-posting with on-the-job search (Burdett-Mortensen), or oligopsony with strategic interaction (BHM). MW constrains markdowns more at low-productivity firms.
Canonical
Robinson (1933)MWHOM Founding static-monopsony book. Marshallian partial-equilibrium textbook articulating monopsony as the buyer-side dual of monopoly (Chapters XI-XIV) and applying it to labor exploitation (Chapters XXV-XXVI): if women's labor supply is more inelastic than men's (e.g., due to mobility or household constraints), firms can extract a higher markdown on women's wages — explaining the men-and-women wage gap without invoking productivity differences. Provides the framework for the modern monopsony-MW results that follow downstream — Stigler (1946) showed that under single-firm monopsony a binding MW reduces the firm's monopsony power and raises BOTH wages AND employment up to the competitive level; Bhaskar-To (1999) extended this to monopsonistic competition with free entry.
Manning (2003)MWHOMEMP Modern canonical monopsony book. Two basic assumptions — labor markets have frictions, and employers (not workers) set wages — together imply that firm-level labor supply is upward-sloping, giving every employer some monopsony power (the within-firm wage markdown survives even with many competitors, i.e., in oligopsony). Chapter 2 develops static, dynamic, and GE wage-posting variants of the model. Chapter 12 shows that the textbook "binding MW raises employment under single-firm monopsony" result does NOT carry over to oligopsony with heterogeneous firms — aggregate employment effects depend on the size of firm-level monopsony markdowns, the elasticity of labor demand, the dispersion of firms' wages, and labor-supply spillovers across firms.
Burdett & Mortensen (1998)MWHOM Founding wage-posting model with on-the-job search. Continuum of firms commit to permanent wage offers; workers search both while unemployed and while employed, sampling firms randomly. Even with identical firms and workers, the unique equilibrium has a continuous wage offer distribution F(w) — providing an answer to Diamond's monopsony paradox (why any wage dispersion exists at all in equilibrium). Section 3 result: a binding MW above the laissez-faire monopsony wage raises BOTH wages and employment in this monopsonistic-competition-from-search-frictions setting.
Bhaskar & To (1999)MWHOMMC Founding monopsonistic-competition model with free entry. Adapts the Salop (1979) product-differentiation circle to labor: n symmetric firms uniformly spaced; workers uniformly distributed with transport cost t over horizontally differentiated job characteristics (t parameterizes the dispersion of worker preferences over non-wage attributes — what gives each firm monopsony power even with many competitors). A binding MW raises firm-level employment but ALSO raises competitor wages, which shifts each firm's labor supply curve inward; this competitor-wage spillover is first-order, so MW cuts profits and triggers exit under free entry. Aggregate effects: industry employment is ambiguous (sign depends on c·t = fixed cost × taste dispersion = structural measure of labor-market distortion); under Cournot product-market competition, MW-induced exit reduces n → raises markup → can raise prices even as industry employment rises, reconciling Card-Krueger's joint NJ observation of higher employment AND higher prices.
Other
Card et al. (2018)MWHOMEMP Modern synthesis + theoretical formalization of firm-level wage premia and labor-market inequality. The empirical "rent sharing" literature documents a positive correlation between firm productivity and wages within narrow industries, traditionally interpreted as firms voluntarily sharing rents with workers through bargaining or fairness norms; this paper synthesizes 22 such studies (mean wage-to-value-added elasticity 0.05-0.15) and adds original analysis on Portuguese matched employer-employee data (Quadros de Pessoal + SABI 2005-2009). Reframes the pattern as monopsonistic wage-setting: workers have idiosyncratic preferences over firms (amenities, commute, fit), so each firm faces a finite-elasticity firm-level labor supply curve and optimally posts a wage with a markdown below MPL — high-productivity firms post higher wages not because they "share" rents voluntarily but because the optimal markdown still leaves a higher wage when MPL is higher. Micro-foundation via BLP (Berry-Levinsohn-Pakes 1995) random-utility discrete-choice methodology from IO; formalizes Robinson's (1933) "classic differentiation" (named by Boal-Ransom 1997) — the idea that workers care about non-wage attributes, giving each firm a captive segment of workers and hence upward-sloping firm-level labor supply.
Engbom & Moser (2022)MWHOMEMPMC Quantitative structural analysis of Brazil's 128% real MW increase 1996-2018, combining RAIS administrative employer-employee data with an extended Burdett-Mortensen wage-posting model (adds unobserved worker heterogeneity, endogenous job creation, MW constraint). Monopsony power arises endogenously from wage-posting + on-the-job search: workers sample firms sequentially while both unemployed and employed; firms commit to a posted wage; a higher wage attracts more applicants and retains workers longer (they are less likely to find a better offer) → upward-sloping firm-level labor supply → finite elasticity → markdown from MPL. Findings: MW explains 45% of the empirical wage-variance decline; spillovers reach throughout the wage distribution (+28% at p10, +6% at p50, +1% at p90); aggregate employment falls only 0.7% because workers reallocate from low- to high-productivity firms; output rises 1.2%, labor productivity 2.8%. Within-industry direction: high-productivity firms benefit (recruit more workers from shrinking competitors); low-productivity firms lose (margins squeezed, vacancies cut).
Berger, Herkenhoff & Mongey (2026)MWHOMEMPMC Synthesis-with-policy-implications paper on labor market power. Articulates three macro mechanisms through which labor market power propagates in concentrated markets: granularity (large firms move market averages mechanically), strategic interaction (share-dependent markdowns from oligopsonistic Nash competition), and misallocation (productive firms restrict hiring → workers stuck at less-productive employers). Applies the "macro approach" to evaluate three regulations: minimum wages (optimal national MW = $8, welfare gain 0.2% of lifetime consumption — but MW captures only ~3% of potential efficiency gains because dominant employers typically pay above MW); noncompete bans (banning raises wages 4%, mostly through spillovers); antitrust enforcement (stricter labor-market antitrust yields large welfare gains). Headline policy stance: MW is broad-based and poorly targeted; targeted policies (antitrust + noncompete bans) dominate for addressing labor market power because rents are concentrated among large firms in concentrated markets.
Dustmann et al. (2022)MWHOMEMPMC Empirical evaluation of Germany's January 2015 nationwide €8.50/hour MW (affected 15% of employees, ~4M jobs, bit much harder in low-wage East Germany). Two identification strategies: individual-level — low-wage treated workers vs high-wage control workers, with the Ashenfelter-Card (1982) adjustment for individual lifecycle wage profiles (low-wage workers' wages tend to grow faster anyway via mean reversion, so a naive DiD would misattribute that growth to the MW); regional-level — Card (1992)-style "fraction-affected" design where each of 401 Kreise (German regions) has a different share of workers initially below the new MW and post-policy outcomes are regressed on this exposure. Findings: MW raised wages substantially without reducing aggregate employment, but induced worker reallocation from small/low-paying/less-productive to large/high-paying/more-productive establishments (worker upgrading explains ~17% of the wage increase; small firms with <3 employees more likely to exit in heavily affected areas; AKM establishment premium and predicted productivity both rise). First direct empirical evidence of the MW-induced reallocation prediction going back to Williamson (1968). Theoretical model (Section V): amenity-based monopsonistic competition à la Card et al. (2018) — workers have idiosyncratic preferences over workplaces, firms heterogeneous in marginal cost; MW drives least-efficient firms out of market and workers are absorbed by more-efficient larger-higher-paying firms.
Aaronson, French & Sorkin (2018)MWHOMEMPMC Empirical + theoretical study of MW effects on the US restaurant industry. Uses QCEW administrative data + state border discontinuity (Dube-Lester-Reich 2010) on five state MW hikes 2001-2006 (CA, IL, MN, OR, DC); three empirical findings: (i) MW hikes raise BOTH restaurant exit AND entry; (ii) the rise is concentrated in chains; (iii) NO change in employment at continuing restaurants. Putty-clay industry-dynamics model: new entrants choose factor mix freely (ex-ante CES) but incumbents are Leontief after capital installation — so MW creates asymmetry between incumbents (fixed labor-intensive mix optimized for OLD MW) and entrants (capital-intensive mix optimized for NEW MW); labor-intensive incumbents exit, capital-intensive entrants enter, continuing firms can't adjust employment, short-run net employment is unchanged. The odd one out in the modern MW C.4 family: NOT monopsony-based — labor markets are perfectly competitive in the model; the friction is in capital vintage. C.4 placement by topical proximity (structural MW paper), not mechanism class.
